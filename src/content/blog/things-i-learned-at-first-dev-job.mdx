I started working at Smartqix last year. Before that, I had built a bunch of side projects and was comfortable coding on my own terms. But joining a team, working on a real codebase with actual users, that's a different thing entirely.

The first month was rough. Not because the work was hard, but because I kept comparing myself to people who had been there for years. They knew where everything was in the codebase. They knew why certain decisions were made three years ago. I was still trying to figure out which branch to push to.

Here are the things that actually mattered.

## nobody expects you to know everything on day one

I spent the first two weeks thinking everyone was silently judging me for asking basic questions. They weren't. Most people are too busy with their own work to care about what you don't know yet. The questions that felt dumb to me were just normal onboarding questions that everyone asks.

The senior devs actually preferred when I asked instead of spending three hours stuck on something they could explain in five minutes. Took me a while to believe that.

## code reviews are not personal attacks

My first pull request got fifteen comments. I thought I had messed up badly. Turned out ten of them were just naming suggestions and formatting preferences. The other five were actual issues that I genuinely hadn't considered.

Nobody was saying my code was bad. They were just trying to make it better before it hit production. Once I stopped reading feedback as criticism and started reading it as free mentorship, code reviews became my favorite part of the workflow.

## the codebase is not as clean as it looks

Every codebase has skeletons. Files that nobody wants to touch. Functions that do three things but are named like they do one thing. Comments that say "TODO: fix this" from 2021.

The difference between a junior and a senior isn't writing cleaner code. It's knowing which messy parts are fine to leave alone and which ones are actually causing problems. Not everything needs to be refactored.

## shipping is the only thing that counts

Side projects taught me this but working at a company reinforced it. Code that sits in a branch is worth zero. Code that ships is worth something, even if it's not perfect.

The first feature I shipped had a bug I knew about. I wanted to fix it before deploying. My manager told me to ship it anyway and fix it in the next update because the fix for the bug would take two hours but the feature was already solving a problem for users right now. Done is better than perfect. Again.

## you grow faster around other people

Working alone on side projects is great for learning at your own pace. But working with other devs accelerates everything. You pick up habits and patterns just by reading their code. You learn communication skills that solo work doesn't teach you. You see how decisions get made when there are multiple opinions in the room.

If you're a student or just starting out, try to find a team to work with. Even if it's an open source project. Even if it's unpaid. The growth curve is completely different when you're building with other people.
