I used to think networking was cringe. Showing up to events with the goal of collecting contacts. Pretending to be interested in someone's startup so they'd remember your name. The whole thing felt fake.

Then I accidentally did it the right way and realized I was just doing it wrong the whole time.

## it's not about what people can do for you

The people who made the biggest difference in my life are people I never asked for anything from. We just talked. About code. About music. About random ideas at 1am in a Discord server.

One of them later referred me to a gig. Another reviewed my code and taught me patterns I still use. Another just believed in me when I didn't believe in myself. None of these things were transactions. They were side effects of genuine connection.

The best networking isn't networking at all. It's just being curious about people and open to conversations. The opportunities follow.

## find your people online

I live in Nigeria. The tech scene here exists but it's not Silicon Valley. Most of the coolest people I've met are online, scattered across different countries and time zones.

Twitter and Discord have been the most useful for this. Not LinkedIn. LinkedIn feels like a job fair. Twitter and Discord feel like hanging out. You see how people think, not just where they work.

The key is to actually talk to people. Not just follow them. Reply to their posts. Share your own stuff. Join conversations about things you actually care about. The connections form naturally when you're not forcing them.

## be the person someone wants to help

If you're just starting out, you're going to be asking for a lot of help. That's fine. Everyone starts there. But the people who get the most help are the ones who make it easy to help them.

Ask specific questions instead of vague ones. Show what you've already tried instead of asking for the full solution. Follow up and tell people when their advice actually helped you solve something. It sounds basic but most people don't do this.

Also, help others when you can. You don't need to be an expert. If you know something that someone else is trying to learn, share it. The people who are generous with their knowledge tend to receive generosity back.

## keep the door open

Some of the best friendships I have started with one conversation and then nothing for six months. Then one person sends a message, the other replies, and suddenly you're collaborating on something.

You don't need to maintain constant contact. Just don't close the door. Reply to their stories. Congratulate them when they ship something. Reach out when you think of them. It takes almost no effort and it compounds over years.

The network you build in your early twenties is the one that carries you through your career. Not in a transactional way. In a human way. These are the people who will tell you about jobs before they're posted. Who will test your app before you launch. Who will tell you honestly when your idea sucks.

Find cool people. Keep them around. It's the best investment you can make.
