For a long time I built things in silence. I'd finish a project, put it on GitHub, and tell maybe two friends about it. Then I'd wonder why nobody was using the thing I spent three months building.

Last year I started posting my stuff online. Nothing fancy. Just screenshots of what I was building, short threads about problems I was solving, and the occasional hot take that got three likes. It changed everything.

## people actually care about the process

I thought people only wanted to see finished products. Polished app screenshots. Clean launch announcements. But the posts that got the most engagement were the messy ones. A bug I couldn't figure out. A UI that looked terrible in the first draft. A deployment that failed at 11pm.

Turns out people relate to the struggle way more than the success. Everyone has polished screenshots. Not everyone shares what it looked like before the polish.

## opportunities come from being visible

The internship I got came from someone seeing my work on Twitter. Not from a job board. Not from a referral. Just someone who followed me, saw what I was building, and reached out when their team needed someone who knew Flutter.

Posting online isn't about going viral. It's about being findable. When someone searches for a person who builds Flutter apps or writes C# backends, your posts show up. Your projects show up. You become an option without even applying.

## it keeps you accountable

There's something about posting "I'm building X this week" that makes you actually build X this week. Even if only ten people see the post, you don't want to be the person who announced something and then disappeared.

I started posting weekly updates on what I was building. Nothing structured. Just a few tweets about what I shipped, what broke, and what I learned. After three months of this, I had shipped more than I had in the previous year. Accountability works even when nobody is holding you accountable.

## ignore the numbers

This is the hard part. You post something you're proud of. Five likes. Two replies. One is spam. It's easy to feel like nobody cares.

But the people who do see it are the right people. One reply from someone who's built something similar is worth more than a hundred likes from people who scrolled past. Quality of engagement is everything.

Also, the numbers lie. I've had posts with twenty likes that led to real conversations and posts with hundreds of likes that led to nothing. The internet is random. Don't let the numbers decide whether you keep posting.

## just start

You don't need a following. You don't need a personal brand. You don't need a content strategy. Post something you built today. Write about a problem you solved. Share a screenshot.

The algorithm rewards consistency more than quality. Five average posts will reach more people than one perfect post. Just start posting and figure out the rest as you go.
